# Final Project: The Mechatronics Ethics & Standards Tabletop Game

**Institution:** Batangas State University – The National Engineering University  
**Course:** MEXE 410: Codes, Standards, and Professional Ethics for MExE  
**Target:** 3rd Year BS Mechatronics Engineering  

---

## 📌 Project Overview
Engineering codes, safety standards, and professional ethics are not just theories; they are active decisions made every day in the industry. For your final project, your group will design, build, and test an original tabletop game (a board game or a card game) that simulates the real-world application of MEXE 410 principles.

Your game must be playable by **2 to 4 players** and must force the players to navigate ethical dilemmas, comply with international standards, and manage engineering risks within a specific Mechatronics sub-field.

---

## 🎲 Topic Assignment & Module Integration
During class, the lecturer will use a "Wheel of Names" to randomly assign each group one of the following specialized industry topics. Your game's theme, hazards, and mechanics must heavily utilize the specific modules assigned to your topic:

**1. Mechatronics Engineering in Research, Design and Development**
* **Module 5.1 & 5.2 (Engineering as Social Experimentation):** These modules dictate how to handle untested prototypes, requiring engineers to act as responsible experimenters, operate with partial knowledge, and secure informed consent from subjects.
* **Module 4 (Research Integrity and Professionalism):** Essential for guidelines on managing intellectual property, proprietary information, and preventing the falsification of test data during the R&D phase.

**2. Mechatronics Engineering in Manufacturing and Process Engineering**
* **Module 2 (Codes and Standards):** This module is the direct reference for factory floor compliance, specifically detailing the ISO 9000 Family (Quality Management to reduce product failures) and the ISO 45000 Family (Occupational Health and Safety to reduce workplace accidents).

**3. Mechatronics Engineering in Robotics and AI**
* **Module 3 (A Review of Future and Ethical Perspectives of Robotics and AI):** Specifically addresses machine ethics, the development of artificial moral agents, and the ethical decision-making frameworks required for autonomous systems.
* **Module 2 (Codes and Standards):** Must be used to reference ISO 10218, which outlines the strict safety requirements for industrial robots and robotic cell integration.

**4. Mechatronics Engineering in Mechanical Engineering**
* **Module 2 (Codes and Standards):** Provides the framework for mechanical design constraints, specifically the consensus standards established by ASME (American Society of Mechanical Engineers).
* **Module 5.1 (Engineering as Social Experimentation):** Essential for understanding mechanical fatigue limits and risk-benefit analysis, utilizing the Challenger Space Shuttle disaster (O-ring failure) as a primary case study.

**5. Mechatronics Engineering in Electronics Engineering**
* **Module 2 (Codes and Standards):** Used as the reference for IEC standards (International Electrotechnical Commission) governing all electronic technologies, as well as local regulations like the Philippine Electronics Code.

**6. Mechatronics Engineering in Electrical Engineering**
* **Module 2 (Codes and Standards):** Completely underpins this topic by providing the NFPA 70 (National Electrical Code) for safe electrical design, NFPA 70E for employee workplace safety (e.g., Lockout/Tagout, arc flash), and the Philippine Electrical Code.

**7. Mechatronics Engineering in Control Systems Engineering**
* **Module 2 (Codes and Standards):** Essential for its coverage of the ISA (International Society of Automation), which provides the technical guidelines for automating and controlling dynamic systems safely.

**8. Mechatronics Engineering in Computer Science and Software Engineering**
* **Module 7 (Global Issues in Engineering Ethics):** Contains the specific section on Computer Ethics. It must be referenced for the "Ten Commandments of Computer Ethics" and rules prohibiting software piracy, hacking, data theft, and the unauthorized use of computing resources.

**9. Mechatronics Engineering in Business**
* **Module 7 (Global Issues in Engineering Ethics):** The primary reference for corporate-level dilemmas, specifically covering Multinational Corporations, Business Ethics, and Environmental Ethics (e.g., sustainable development, managing host vs. local country practices).
* **Module 6.1 & 6.2 (NSPE Code and Key Concepts):** Provides the rules of practice for business operations, such as avoiding Conflicts of Interest, ensuring objective vendor selection, and maintaining fidelity to clients.

**10. Mechatronics Engineering in Academe**
* **Module 4 (Research Integrity and Professionalism):** The definitive guide for academic topics, outlining the "Eight Cardinal Rules of Academic Integrity" and strictly defining offenses like plagiarism and scientific misconduct.
* **Module 6.3 (ABET Code of Ethics):** Provides the professional standards for engineering educators and institutional programs.

## 🛠️ Game Design Requirements
* **Player Count:** The game must strictly support 2, 3, or 4 players.
* **Educational Integration:** The core mechanics must involve applying ISO/IEC standards, local codes, or solving ethical conflicts. For example, players might lose points for bypassing a safety code or win by successfully certifying an industrial tool.
* **Game Mechanics:** You are free to design any system that emphasizes precision and industrial utility. You might use custom dice, such as a primary die displaying faces like "1/1, 2/2, 3/3" where the first number dictates a base compliance score and the second half dictates an integration score. Alternatively, you could develop card mechanics that allow players to chain multiple consecutive engineering actions or replicate successful component designs on the board to build a specialized engine.

## 📂 Deliverables & Submission Process

### 1. The GitHub Repository (Documentation)
Create a new GitHub repository named `Final_MEXE410_Section_Group#_2026`. This repository must contain:
* **The Rulebook:** A detailed `README.md` explaining the objective, setup, turn order, and win conditions of your game.
* **The Developer Log:** A section in the `README.md` documenting your creation process, including photos of your prototype development.
* **The Video Playtest:** A link (YouTube or Google Drive) to a recorded video of your group doing a live test run of the game, proving the mechanics work.

### 2. The Physical Game (Presentation Day)
You must bring the fully completed, physical version of your board game or card game to class. Ensure you have all necessary pieces, cards, dice, and a printed rulebook. 

### 3. The Live Playtest Evaluation
Presentation day will not be a standard slideshow. We will set up dedicated playtest stations. Your group will act as the "Game Masters." You must teach and run a live game session where the lecturer, visiting faculty members, or students from other groups sit down and play your game.
